Introduction

      Chess, a game of strategy and skill, has enthralled players for centuries. Whether you're a beginner or looking to sharpen your skills, understanding the fundamentals of chess is essential. In this article, I provide a comprehensive introduction to chess, covering the basic rules, the movement of pieces, and essential strategies to set you on your path to becoming a formidable chess player.

The Chessboard and Setup

A chessboard consists of 64 squares, alternately light and dark in color. Each player starts with 16 pieces: one king, one queen, two rooks, two knights, two bishops, and eight pawns. The board is set up with the rooks placed in the corners, followed by the knights, then the bishops, the queen on her own color, and the king beside her.

The Chess Pieces and Their Moves

The Rook

The rook moves in straight lines, horizontally or vertically, any number of squares.

The Bishop

The bishop moves diagonally, any number of squares. Each player has two bishops, one on a light square and one on a dark square. This means that you can only attack with one of them on either white squares and black sqares.

The Queen

The queen is the most powerful piece. It can move any number of squares in any direction: horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. You can think of her as both a rook and bishop combined.

The Knight

The knight moves in an L-shape, either two squares horizontally and one vertically, or one square horizontally and two vertically. It is the only piece that can "jump" over other pieces.

The Pawn

Depending on the color, pawns move forward one square, but only captures diagonally. On their first move, they have the option to move forward two squares. Pawns are the only pieces that can promote.

The King

The king is the most important piece. It can move one square in any direction, horizontally, vertically, or diagonally, as long as they are not attacked.

Basic Rulesets

Taking Turns

In chess, players take turns moving pieces. The first move belongs to white.

Attacking a Piece

To attack, the piece movement must colide with another piece of opposite color. On colision, the movement of the initial piece stops. Same thing happens if the piece movement colides with a piece of the same color.

Knight "Jump"

When we say the knight can "jump" over pieces we refer to the fact that if the knight is boxed in, it can still escape.

King Movement Restrictions

In the game of chess, kings can not be captured, instead they remain on the board until the end of the game. This means that if you move the king on a spot where it can be captured, it's considered an "illegal move", same goes if you move a piece that is protecting the king.

Point System

The point system refers to the "value" of the piece: Pawn - 1p, Knight and Bishop - 3p, Rook - 5p and Queen - 9p. It is usually not recomended to sacrifice a piece with more worth to a piece with less worth.

En Passant

En Passant is a special chess move that can occur under specific conditions. First, an opponent's pawn moves two squares forward from its starting position. If the moving pawn lands beside an opposite color pawn on an adjacent file (column), the en passant rule is applied. You can only capture on the first turn of the rule.

Castle

Castling is a defensive chess move that allows the king to move to a safer position while simultaneously developing one of the rooks. The conditions for castling are as follows:
  1. The king and the chosen rook must not have been previously moved during the game.
  2. The squares between the king and the chosen rook must be unoccupied.
  3. There should be no pieces attacking the squares the king passes over or the squares adjacent to the king's final position.
There are two types of castling: Kingside castling and Queenside castling. In Kingside castling the king moves two squares towards the rook on its right, and the rook jumps over the king, landing on the square next to it. In Queenside castling, the king moves two squares towards the rook on its left, and the rook jumps over the king, landing on the square next to it.

Pawn Promote

Pawns can promote if they reach the last row. Whenever a pawn promotes, it can transform in a bishop, knight, rook or queen of the same color. Usually it's for the best to transform in a queen, as it is the most powerful piece in chess.

Check

     The king is in check whenever a piece of opposite color attacks it. When the king is in check, you can only move the king, a piece that can protect the king or a piece that can capture the attacker.

Checkmate

     The goal in chess is to checkmate your opponent's king. Checkmate occurs when the king is under attack (in "check") and cannot escape capture on the next move. The game is won by the player who successfully checkmates their opponent's king.

Draws

There are multiple ways to draw in chess, first one being the absense of material. This means you don't have enough pieces to checkmate the enemy king. The second way to draw is the absense of activity, meaning you have not capured a piece or moved a pawn in over 50 turns. The third way to draw is by cutting of all the avalable moves of the opposite color. If it's their turn and they can't move anything, then it's a draw.

Strategies

Developing Pieces

In chess, the advantage goes to the person who has the most developed pieces. A developed piece is a piece wich has a purpose. You can give pieces purpose if you get it out of the bottom row. The more space they can freely move, the better. The order in wich you should develop pieces is: Knight, Bishop, Queen and Rook, however, it is good to develop any piece whenever you get the opportunity.

Safe King

The king is the most important piece in chess, therefore, you have to keep it safe at all times. To keep your king safe, you must prevent checks as often as possible. A good strategy is castling.

2 Rook Checkmate

The 2 Rook checkmate is the easiest checkmate. To checkmate with 2 rooks, you have to trap the king by blocking off one of the sides, then alternate between rooks until you reach the margin.

King and Queen Checkmate

The King and Queen checkmate is a bit more finiky. To checkmate with a Queen, you have to hold the enemy king in "knight opposition", meaning you have to treat the queen as a knight that's always giving checks. Your goal is to bring the enemy king to a corner, where you'll have a bunch of opportunities to checkmate. Just be careful not to draw!

Forks

"Fork" is a term used in chess refering to attacking two pieces at once. They are usually done with knights, however it's not unusual to do forks with other pieces.

Skewers

"Skewers" are nothing other than tying a lower value piece to a more valuable piece. This way, the lower value piece is unable to move, otherwise you will capture the higher value piece.
